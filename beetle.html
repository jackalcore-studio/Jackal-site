Chemistry creature in development

Beetle

Beetle is a chemistry-focused AI being built to make one of the hardest STEM fields feel less like a locked cabinet full of unlabeled glassware. It is part tutor, part lookup tool, and part tiny lab creature that knows when to say, “that depends.”

The goal is not to make chemistry look simple. The goal is to make chemistry approachable without flattening it into lies. Beetle should be able to explain a compound to a beginner, talk through structure and bonding with an intermediate learner, and stop wasting time when the user clearly already knows the basics.

What Beetle is for

Beetle is being designed as a chemistry helper with a spine. It should help users understand formulas, structures, bonding, solubility, oxidation states, periodic trends, reaction plausibility, and the annoying little condition-dependent details that make chemistry chemistry.

A lot of AI tools answer chemistry questions with confident mush. Beetle’s job is to be slower, clearer, and less willing to hallucinate a compound into existence just because the user typed something that sounded science-flavored.

Source-backed lookup

Beetle is being wired to check chemical identity data before speaking too loudly. Formula, molecular weight, IUPAC name, SMILES, and other basics should come from actual lookup instead of model vibes.

Adaptive teaching

A beginner should get plain language. An advanced user should get chemistry. Beetle’s job is to notice the difference and adjust without being condescending.

Conditions matter

Concentration, solvent, temperature, structure, oxidation state, and physical form can change the answer. Beetle is allowed to be annoying about that. Lovingly.

What Beetle should be good at

Compound identity Looking up what a chemical is before explaining it: formula, mass, names, identifiers, and structural representations when available.
Level-matched explanations Explaining chlorine differently to a middle-school student, a high-school student, a first-year chemistry student, and someone who casually says “ligand field splitting.”
Reaction plausibility Talking through whether a reaction makes chemical sense, what factors matter, and what information is missing before pretending there is one universal answer.
Concept repair Helping users untangle misconceptions without making them feel stupid for asking. Chemistry is already rude enough on its own.

What Beetle is not

Beetle is not meant to be a homework vending machine, a drug synthesis assistant, a pyrotechnics cookbook, or a creature that tells people to mix random household chemicals because the internet got bored. It can explain risk, identity, structure, and theory. It should not hand people a dangerous procedure wrapped in a cute interface.

It also is not meant to replace actual thought. Beetle should help users build a mental model, not just hand them a polished paragraph and call that learning.

Current status

Beetle is still in development. The public chat page is only a preview for now, while the real chemistry brain is being tested separately with PubChem lookup and local model reasoning. The current focus is making Beetle better at identifying compounds, asking for missing chemical context, and adapting to how much chemistry the user actually knows.

Translation: the beetle is alive, but still under the leaf. Do not tap the glass too hard.
